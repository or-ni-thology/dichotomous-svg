### WHY

A dichotomous key is a ladder of either/ors that a naturalist climbs down until one bird remains. This instrument turns the ladder over. Rather than reading a wader down to a name, the user builds a wader up from its characters and reads off the name it cannot help but be.
Set up to describe the Scolopacidae of the North Pennines, the concept could in theory work for the Threskiornithidae of Los Llanos with some minor adjustments to bill curvature. 

The tool is *very useful* for creating images for games of Scolopictionary.
 
### WHAT

- 8 body shapes, 10 bill types, 7 bill colours, 9 head patterns, 3 eye positions
- 4 leg lengths, 8 leg colours, 5 tail types (including Jack Snipe whole-body bob)
- 8 plumage tones with colour wash rendering
- 6 habitat backgrounds: moorland, river margin, boggy pool, woodland floor, wet hay meadow

### USEFUL BIRD INFORMATION

The language of wader identification is anatomical precision. Here is the vocabulary of bill, crown, and flight-feather, arranged from head to tail:

- **Head**
    - **Culmen** — the dorsal ridge of the upper mandible (bill length measured from here)
    - **Gonys** — the ridge of the lower mandible
    - **Lore / Loral stripe** — between bill base and eye
    - **Supercilium** — eyebrow stripe (above the lore)
    - **Median crown-stripe** — pale stripe along crown centre
    - **Lateral crown-stripe** — pale stripes either side of dark crown
    - **Orbital ring** — bare skin immediately encircling the eye
    - **Iris** — eye colour
- **Body — Upperparts**
    - **Nape** — back of neck
    - **Mantle** — upper back
    - **Scapulars** — shoulder feathers, overlying wing base
    - **Rump** — lower back, above tail
    - **Uppertail coverts** — feathers covering tail base above
- **Body — Underparts**
    - **Throat, breast, belly, flanks, vent**
    - **Undertail coverts** — feathers covering tail base below
    - **Pectoral band/patches** — breast-side markings
- **Wing (folded)**
    - **Lesser coverts** — innermost row
    - **Median coverts** — middle row
    - **Greater coverts** — outermost row (their tips often form a wingbar)
    - **Alula** — small feathers at the carpal joint (wrist)
    - **Tertials** — innermost "secondary-like" feathers, usually covering folded primaries
    - **Primary projection** — how far the primaries extend beyond the tertial tips at rest (important in separating similar species)
- **Wing (open/in flight)**
    - **Primaries** — outer 10 flight feathers
    - **Secondaries** — inner flight feathers
    - **Wingbar** — pale bar formed by covert tips or feather bases
    - **Wing panel** — broader pale area
    - **Underwing** — pale or dark? Barred? Critical in flight ID
- **Tail & Bare Parts**
    - **Rectrices** (sing. rectrix) — the tail feathers themselves
    - **Tarsus** — the "lower leg" (actually fused tarsometatarsus)
    - **Tibia** — upper leg
    - **Hallux** — the hind toe

# The Residents & Breeders


## Numenius arquata — Eurasian Curlew

**Standing**

Our largest Scolopacidae and an unmistakable one — if you can forget the bill for a moment and learn it fresh. Length 50–60cm; bill alone 10–15cm in female, shorter in male — the primary sexual dimorphism of the species. Bill extremely long, decurved through its distal half, base pale pinkish-flesh grading smoothly to a dark, flexible tip (packed with Herbst's corpuscles for tactile detection). Supercilium indistinct, pale buff. Crown pale buff with dense dark brown shaft-streaks. Mantle and scapulars dark brown with pale buff notching and streaking, giving a warm brown marbled effect. Tertials similarly patterned, overlying secondaries on the folded wing. Primary projection moderate to long. Underparts pale buff to white; breast and flanks with moderate dark brown streaking, belly whiter. Undertail coverts whitish, finely streaked. Tarsus long, blue-grey. Iris dark brown.

**Flying**

Wingbeats rather slow and deep — almost gull-like in their unhurried authority. Wingspan to 100cm. Upperwing uniformly brown, no wingbar. Rump white, extending as a bold white wedge up the lower back — the field-mark that catches the eye at distance. Underwing pale buff-white with fine dark barring. Outer primaries dark brown. The call announces the bird before anything visual resolves: the cascading, liquid, rising *cur-leee* — one of the most emotionally freighted sounds in the British uplands.

**Nesting**

Shallow scrape on open moorland, rough grass or heather, sparsely lined with plant stems and leaves. Eggs: typically 4, strongly pyriform (pear-shaped — essential for rolling in circle rather than rolling away), 55 × 38mm, ground colour olive-green to warm olive-brown, blotched and spotted dark brown and grey. Extraordinarily well camouflaged. Incubation shared by both sexes, 26–30 days. Chicks nidifugous (leave nest almost immediately), cryptically downy.

**Habitat & Behaviour**

Blanket bog, wet heath, upland rush-pasture. Feeds by deep probing with the decurved bill — reaching invertebrates inaccessible to shorter-billed species. Display flight involves slow, soaring wingbeats building into the full bubbling song. Gregarious in winter, departing to estuaries; the loss of that mournful call over the moor is the sound of summer ending.

## Gallinago gallinago — Common Snipe

**Standing**

Medium, 25–27cm, but the bill commands disproportionate attention: straight, extremely long (6–7cm), laterally compressed toward the sensitive, flexible, prehensile-tipped distal third. Bill base greenish-flesh, tip dark. The head pattern is diagnostic: four pale stripes — a pale median crown-stripe flanked by dark lateral crown-stripes, themselves flanked by broad pale supercilia — creating a longitudinally striped helmet. Eye remarkably large, set high on the head (enabling binocular vision both forwards and above, crucial for a bill-in-mud feeder watching for raptors). Mantle with four prominent pale buff scapular lines running longitudinally. Scapulars dark brown with broad pale buff outer webs. Tertials dark with pale notching. Primary projection moderate. Breast warm buff-brown with dark streaking; flanks with dark brown barring; belly white. Tarsus greenish-yellow. Iris dark brown.

**Flying**

Escape flight: explosive from almost underfoot, with a rasping *scaap* call, followed by characteristic towering zig-zag ascent before levelling and dropping suddenly back into cover. Wings pointed. In sustained flight: white trailing edge to secondaries visible as a wingbar. Rump dark brown (not white — important Woodcock distinction). Outer rectrices rufous-orange, tipped white — visible in display. Underwing barred.

**Nesting**

Deep cup of coarse grasses and sedge, concealed within a grass tussock or rush clump, often with a canopy of overhanging vegetation. Eggs: 4, pyriform, olive to greenish-brown ground, blotched and spotted dark brown, markings concentrated at the broad end. c.39 × 28mm. Incubation primarily female, 18–20 days. Chicks nidifugous.

**Habitat & Behaviour**

Wet moorland, blanket bog with *Sphagnum*, rush-pasture, marshy flushes. Feeds by rhythmic "sewing machine" probing in soft mud — the bill tip finding invertebrates by touch rather than sight. The famous aerial "drumming": not vocal — the mechanical sound of the outer rectrices (stiffened, specialized) vibrating as the male dives steeply in display, producing that resonant, goat-like bleating that fills summer evenings on the bog.

## Scolopax rusticola — Eurasian Woodcock

**Standing**

33–35cm. Bill long, straight, pale pinkish at base, dark at tip; the tip region richly endowed with Herbst's corpuscles — the bill essentially "listens" through the soil for earthworms. The head pattern is unique among Palaearctic waders: not longitudinally striped but transversely barred — alternating bands of black and warm buff-orange across the crown and nape. Large eye set extraordinarily high and far back on the skull, providing near-360° visual field — an adaptation to feeding head-down. Mantle and scapulars a masterwork of cryptic patterning: rufous-chestnut, black, grey and buff in complex vermiculation, replicating the texture of dead leaves almost perfectly. Underparts pale buff with dark transverse barring. Tarsus pinkish-grey.

**Flying**

Broad, rounded wings (unlike most Scolopacidae's pointed wings). Primarily crepuscular and nocturnal. The roding display: males fly regular circuits along woodland edges and rides at dusk and dawn, low and methodical, bill angled downward, wings beating with distinctive frog-like undulation, uttering alternating grunting *orwick* calls and thin high *tsiwick* squeaks. Richly barred upperparts catch the failing light. Flushes explosively from ground cover with wing-clatter.

**Nesting**

Shallow scrape among dead leaves on woodland floor, under bracken or bramble — the nest is the leaf litter. Eggs: 4, pale buff to cream ground, blotched and spotted warm reddish-brown. c.44 × 33mm. Incubation by female alone, 20–23 days. Notable behaviour: the female will carry chicks gripped between her thighs in flight when disturbed — one of the most remarkable parental transport behaviours in British birds.

**Habitat & Behaviour**

In the North Pennines: moorland edges, boggy woodland margins, wet birch and alder carr, *Sphagnum* flushes with adjacent scrub cover. Feeds by deep probing in soft soil for earthworms. Almost entirely crepuscular; by day, freezes among leaf litter and is essentially invisible. A ghost bird.

## Actitis hypoleucos — Common Sandpiper

**Standing**

Small, 19–21cm, neat and compact. Bill straight, medium-length, dark brownish above with pale base to lower mandible. Supercilium whitish. Crown and upperparts uniform warm olive-brown with subtle dark shaft-streaking. Tertials show faint darker barring. Key standing field-mark: a white "shoulder spur" — a notch of white breast-side cutting upward between the brown pectoral patches and the folded wing. Breast-sides warm brown forming a pectoral patch on each side; white below. Undertail coverts with fine dark barring. Tarsus and legs yellowish-green. Orbital ring pale. Iris dark brown.

**Flying**

Unmistakable flight: low over water, alternating rapid shallow wingbeats with stiff-winged, bowed-wing glides — a flickering, mechanical action unlike any other wader. Wingbar white and distinct along secondaries and inner primaries. Rump dark. Outer rectrices barred brown and white. Call diagnostic: piping *twee-wee-wee-wee*, rapid.

**Nesting**

Near water. Shallow scrape lined with plant debris, hidden in vegetation — grass tussock, under bracken, among exposed roots — usually within a few metres of a stream or reservoir edge. Eggs: 4, pyriform, creamy-buff to pale greenish, with bold reddish-brown spots and blotches, often concentrated at broader end. c.36 × 26mm. Incubation shared, 21–23 days.

**Habitat & Behaviour**

Almost always associated with running or standing water: upland streams, river shingle, reservoir margins, ghyll sides. The perpetual teetering — rhythmic bobbing of the hindquarters — is diagnostic at any range and in any viewing condition. Territorial pairs defend stretches of stream vigorously.

## Calidris alpina — Dunlin

**Standing (breeding)**

Small, 16–20cm. Bill black, medium-length, with characteristic slight droop at the tip — subtly but reliably decurved distally. In breeding plumage: crown rufous-chestnut streaked dark. Mantle and scapulars rufous-chestnut with black feather centres — a scaled, glowing pattern. Breast white with dark brown streaking. **Diagnostic: bold black belly patch** — the only North Pennines Scolopacidae with this feature, an absolute clincher. Tarsus black. Iris dark.

**Standing (non-breeding/juvenile)**

Upperparts grey-brown with pale feather edges. Underparts white with diffuse grey-brown suffusion on breast. Black belly absent. Much drabber — but the slightly drooped bill tip still tells.

**Flying**

Wingbar white, distinct. Rump: dark central stripe flanked by white sides. Tail dark grey. Flight somewhat erratic, fast. In winter flocks: spectacular coordinated aerial murmurations.

**Nesting**

Shallow scrape in short vegetation, *Sphagnum* moss, or cotton-grass (*Eriophorum*). Eggs: 4, pyriform, pale greenish to buff-olive, with warm reddish-brown blotching. c.35 × 24mm. Incubation shared, 21–22 days.

**Habitat & Behaviour**

Blanket bog, wet heath, *Sphagnum* mire, cotton-grass moorland. The North Pennines represents the southern upland edge of the breeding range in England — a genuinely significant population. Feeds with rapid "sewing machine" probing action in wet ground.

## Tringa totanus — Common Redshank

**Standing**

Medium, 27–29cm. Bill medium-length, straight: red at base grading to dark tip — instantly distinctive. Tarsus and legs red — the diagnostic field-mark at any range in any season. Upperparts warm brown, profusely spotted and streaked dark brown. Underparts white; breast and flanks heavily streaked dark brown. Orbital ring reddish. Eye dark.

**Flying**

The signature feature: very broad white trailing edge to the secondaries, striking and unmistakable in any light. White rump. Underwing white. Wingbeats rapid. Call: yelping, liquid *teu-teu-teu* or more excited yodelling — persistently vocal.

**Nesting**

Ground nest in rank grass, rush, sedge — often woven into a tunnel of surrounding vegetation for concealment. Eggs: 4, pyriform, pale buff to stone-cream ground, blotched reddish-brown. c.44 × 31mm. Incubation shared, 23–25 days.

**Habitat & Behaviour**

Upland rush-pasture, wet meadows, moorland pool margins, boggy ground. The "sentinel of the marshes": invariably the first species to detect a human or raptor and raise the alarm, its persistent *teu-teu-teu* alerting every other wader for considerable distance. Stands tall and watchful. In the North Pennines, strongly associated with traditionally managed damp hay-meadows — a reliable indicator of good habitat quality.

# The Passers-Through

*Seasonal visitors. Harder to know, thrilling to draw.*

## Lymnocryptes minimus — Jack Snipe

**Standing**

Small, 17–19cm — noticeably smaller than Common Snipe, though isolation makes size hard to judge. Bill shorter than Common Snipe but still proportionately long. **Critical head pattern difference**: two pale stripes (the supercilia), dark between them — no pale median crown-stripe (Common Snipe has four stripes including a median crown-stripe). Mantle dark with two pairs of very prominent pale scapular lines — these have a subtle iridescent purple-green gloss in good light. Underparts buff with dark breast streaking.

**Flying**

When finally flushed (it will have let you get embarrassingly close first), flies silently, in a direct, less erratic line, and drops back quickly — quite unlike Common Snipe's explosive *scaap* and towering zigzag. Shorter tail; the rufous outer rectrices of Common Snipe are absent.

**Habitat & Behaviour**

Autumn and winter visitor to wet flushes, *Sphagnum* bogs, marshy moorland edges. A bird of extraordinary crypticity — will sit perfectly still while you stand a metre away, relying completely on camouflage. When feeding, bobs rhythmically — the whole body pumping, rather than just the tail (Common Sandpiper) or the rear (Common Snipe's drumming context). This bobbing is unique and visible at range.

## Numenius phaeopus — Whimbrel

**Standing**

Medium-large, 40–46cm — appreciably smaller than Curlew but easy to confuse at distance. Bill long, decurved, but noticeably shorter than Curlew's, with a more pronounced "kink" at the mid-point rather than a uniform curve. **Head pattern diagnostic**: three pale crown-stripes — pale median crown-stripe flanked by dark lateral crown-stripes bordered by broad pale supercilia — creating a boldly striped crown quite unlike Curlew's indistinctly streaked head. Mantle and scapulars dark brown, pale-notched. Underparts pale buff to white with dark breast and flank streaking. Tarsus blue-grey.

**Flying**

Similar general impression to Curlew but wingbeats slightly snappier. White rump. **Call absolutely diagnostic**: a rapid, rippling, seven-note whistle — *ti-ti-ti-ti-ti-ti-ti* — one of the most distinctive calls in the wader repertoire; you'll never forget it once heard.

**Habitat & Behaviour**

Spring passage: April–May. Autumn passage: July–August. Flies over moorland, often calling. Usually in small groups. The overlapping period with Curlew on the moor makes the head pattern and call all the more important.

---

## Tringa ochropus — Green Sandpiper

**Standing**

Medium, 21–24cm. Bill straight, dark, medium-length. Upperparts very dark olive-brown, heavily spotted white — giving a spangled, starling-like effect. This darkness is the keynote of the species. Underparts white; breast with heavy dark brown streaking — the contrast between dark breast-streaking and white belly is sharp. Legs greenish. Orbital ring white. Eye dark.

**Flying**

The key flight view: dark above, then brilliant white rump and uppertail coverts erupt — the contrast almost shocking. Rectrices white with dark barring. **Underwing very dark, almost blackish** — this separates it from Wood Sandpiper (pale underwing) at any range. Call: liquid, upward-inflected *weet-a-weet* or *weet-weet-weet*, given as it towers away.

**Remarkable Note**

On the continent: uniquely among European waders, regularly uses old nests of other species in trees — Song Thrush, Mistle Thrush, Wood Pigeon nests, squirrel dreys — at heights of 2–10m.

**Habitat & Behaviour**

Passage and winter visitor to wet flushes, boggy runnels, ditch edges, reservoir margins. Tends to be solitary or in very small numbers. Flushes explosively with a towering, call-giving exit.

## Tringa nebularia — Common Greenshank

**Standing**

Medium-large, 30–34cm. Bill long, slightly upturned at tip, grey-green with dark tip — this gentle upward curve is quite distinctive. Legs/tarsus yellowish-green, long. Upperparts in non-breeding grey-brown with paler feather edges; mantle and scapulars darker with more pattern in breeding. Head and neck white with dark streaking. Underparts white; breast with fine dark streaking in breeding plumage. An elegant, long-legged, pale-looking wader.

**Flying**

White rump extends as a wedge up the back (like Curlew, unlike Redshank's white rump). **No wingbar** (critical: Redshank has a very broad white wingbar; Greenshank has none). Wings dark above. Call: resonant, ringing three-note *tew-tew-tew* — fuller and more carrying than Redshank.

**Habitat & Behaviour**

Passage migrant, spring and autumn. Moorland pools, reservoir edges, flushes. Often wades deeply, pursuing fish or invertebrates with active chasing behaviour unusual among waders.

# The Rare & Glorious

*Unlikely but possible. Bonus round material.*

## Tringa glareola — Wood Sandpiper

**Standing**

Small-medium, 19–21cm. Bill straight, dark, medium-short. **Supercilium long, white, very prominent** — more obvious than Green Sandpiper's. Upperparts olive-brown, heavily spotted and notched whitish-buff — similar to Green Sandpiper but spots typically larger and paler, giving a slightly brighter, more "freckled" appearance. Underparts white; breast with diffuse brown streaking fading cleanly into white belly. Legs yellow, noticeably longer and more elegant than Green Sandpiper.

**Flying**

**Pale underwing** — the critical distinction from Green Sandpiper (dark underwing). White rump. Tail with dark barring. Call: high, rapid *chiff-chiff-chiff* or *chip-chip-chip*.

**Status**

Rare vagrant from Scandinavian breeding grounds to North Pennines.

## Calidris pugnax — Ruff

**Standing (breeding male)**

Medium, 26–32cm male (female "Reeve" considerably smaller at 20–25cm — remarkable sexual size dimorphism). Bill medium, slightly drooped, dark. The Ruff in breeding plumage is the most outrageously polymorphic bird likely to be encountered in these hills. Bare facial skin: orange-red caruncles/wattles around bill base and eye. **Ruff and ear-tufts**: the extravagant neck frill and head plumage is essentially individually unique — white, buff, orange-chestnut, black, barred, plain, iridescent — each male carrying a different livery. A bird that seems to deny the concept of species-typical appearance.

**Standing (non-breeding/juvenile — most likely North Pennines appearance)**

Upperparts grey-brown with pale feather edges, giving a neat scaly pattern. Underparts white, breast with diffuse buff wash. Bill dark. Legs orange or yellowish — the leg colour often the best non-breeding field-mark.

**Flying**

Oval white patches at sides of rump/uppertail coverts — one on each side, contrasting with dark central rump stripe. Narrow wingbar. Flight rather languid and slightly loose-winged.

**Habitat & Behaviour**

Passage migrant over moorland, wet fields, flushes. Usually in small groups. The dramatic breeding plumage males are occasional but unforgettable — a genuine Pataphysical event.

# The Accidentals & Arguables

*Birds that have no business being on the moor. And sometimes land anyway, briefly, in the rain, like a dispatch from an impossible distance.*

## Limosa limosa — Black-tailed Godwit

**Standing**

Large, 36–44cm, and possessed of an architectural elegance that makes the other waders look like they haven't quite finished growing. Bill very long — 7–11cm — and **perfectly straight** along its entire length, pinkish-orange at the base graduating cleanly to a dark tip; this absolute straightness is the standing distinction from the Bar-tailed at any range.

In breeding plumage: head, neck and breast suffused with **rich rufous-chestnut**, deepening in the male almost to brick-orange; belly white with dense dark brown barring on the flanks — this barred flank pattern is critical. Scapulars dark brown with rufous-chestnut notching at feather edges.

Non-breeding plumage is a different creature entirely — grey-brown above, whitish below, plain and almost Greenshank-like — but the straight bill and extraordinary leg length remain the anchors. Tarsus very long, dark grey-black. The overall standing impression: a wader drawn by someone who wanted to see how far they could push *attenuated*.

**Flying**

Spectacular and nearly unmistakable. **Bold white wingbar** runs across the full length of secondaries and inner primaries — broader and more striking than any other wader on this list. **White rump**. Then the **black tail** — that clean, full, black terminal band against white is the clinching field-mark, legible at considerable distance and in poor light. Legs project well beyond tail tip. Underwing white. The whole flight impression is of a bird that has resolved its design entirely around contrast.

**Notes**

Formerly bred in the East Anglian fens; now a handful of pairs cling on at managed reserves. Largely a passage migrant inland; the North Pennines offers moorland pools and wet rush-pasture that occasionally tempt spring birds north of their usual route. *Limosa limosa islandica* — the Icelandic subspecies, darker and richer in breeding plumage — is the subspecies most likely over these hills.

## Limosa lapponica — Bar-tailed Godwit

**Standing**

Medium-large, 33–41cm — slightly stockier and shorter-legged than Black-tailed, the difference subtle but real. Bill long, but with a **gentle, consistent upward curve** — not dramatic, easily missed on a single bird, but reliable when you're looking for it.

In breeding male: the underparts are **entirely deep brick-red, unbarred** — *no dark barring on the flanks*, which is the critical separation from the Black-tailed's barred flanks; some males are so richly coloured they look almost lacquered. Females breeding: much paler below, a washed-out peachy tone rather than the male's intensity.

Non-breeding: brown, streaked above; whitish, streaked on breast below. Legs dark, shorter than Black-tailed. A stockier, more purposeful-looking bird on the ground — less willowy, more engineered.

**Flying**

**No wingbar** — the crucial separation from Black-tailed in flight. Say it a few times: *no wingbar.* Rump white but streaked and barred brown — not the clean white of Black-tailed. Tail barred brown and white, not black-banded. Wings entirely dark above. The absence of pattern is the pattern.

**The Extraordinary Fact**

*The Bar-tailed Godwit holds the confirmed record for the longest non-stop migratory flight of any bird on Earth* — up to **11,700km from Alaska to New Zealand without landing, without feeding, without sleeping in any conventional sense**, over nine to eleven days above the Pacific. The *baueri* subspecies, to be taxonomically precise. Their digestive organs and kidneys **shrink** before departure to reduce weight; their entire body becomes engine. A North Pennines sighting is a bird that may have crossed the planet in one breath.
